## Summary

Netflix has decisively won the streaming market and, just as importantly, has turned that scale into real profit and cash. Revenue reached **$45.2bn in FY2025 (+16%)**, operating margin expanded to roughly **30%** (from ~18% three years earlier), and free cash flow grew to about **$9.5bn**. This is a high-quality, cash-generative franchise with genuine pricing power.

The difficulty is the price, not the business. At ~$110 the market already appears to be paying for the most optimistic outcome. A discounted-cash-flow valuation built on management's own near-term guidance produces a **base-case value of ~$52 per share**, and a comparable-company cross-check lands close to **~$50**. Only an aggressive **bull case (~$115)** — combining sustained high-teens growth, free-cash-flow margins in the low-30s, *and* a lower cost of capital — reaches today's share price. In other words, the stock is priced for perfection, leaving little margin of safety.

## The business

Netflix monetises a single content library across roughly 190 countries, now serving about **325 million paid memberships**. Three changes reshaped the economics since 2022. First, the crackdown on password sharing converted free viewers into paying members. Second, a lower-priced advertising tier opened a second, higher-margin revenue stream that surpassed **$1.5bn** in FY2025 and is still early. Third, operating discipline turned years of content-driven cash burn into substantial free cash flow, so the content slate is increasingly self-funded. Together these explain both the margin expansion and the cash-flow inflection.

## Valuation

The core valuation is a five-year DCF using a free-cash-flow-margin approach (more reliable than a textbook build-up for a content-heavy business), mid-year discounting, and a Gordon-growth terminal value. The cost of capital is **~10.8%**, derived from CAPM; because Netflix is roughly 99% equity-funded, the weighted cost of capital sits just below the ~10.9% cost of equity. The base case anchors to management's FY2026 guidance (~$51bn revenue, ~31.5% operating-margin target) and tapers growth toward the broader economy while free-cash-flow margins continue to rise. Terminal value represents about **74%** of enterprise value — high, but normal for a still-growing company and within a defensible range.

A comparable-company analysis reinforces the picture. Netflix trades at roughly **10× sales** and **42× earnings**, versus a peer-group median near **2× sales**. That premium is justified only by Netflix's superior growth and margins; applying peer medians to Netflix's own metrics implies a value (~$50) well below the current price.

## Scenarios

The valuation is most sensitive to the discount rate and, secondarily, to long-run growth and margins. Moving the WACC from ~10.8% toward 8.5% nearly doubles the per-share value, which is precisely why the bull case is the only scenario that reaches the market price.

| Scenario | Key assumptions | Value / share |
|----------|-----------------|--------------:|
| Bear | Slower growth, ~11.5% WACC | ~$35 |
| Base | Guidance-led, ~10.8% WACC | ~$52 |
| Bull | High-teens growth, low-30s FCF margin, ~8.5% WACC | ~$115 |

## Risks and catalysts

The principal risk is valuation itself: with the bull case already in the price, any disappointment has limited downside support. Beyond that, Netflix faces a costly global content arms race against well-capitalised rivals, slowing membership growth as penetration rises, and execution risk on scaling advertising. On the other side, advertising inflecting from a small base, continued pricing power and paid-sharing monetisation, ongoing operating leverage, and new surfaces such as live events and games could all push results above the base case.

**Key caveat — not modelled.** In December 2025 Netflix announced an all-cash acquisition of Warner Bros. (approximately $72bn equity value). This would materially change Netflix's debt, share count, and growth/margin profile. This memo deliberately values Netflix on a standalone basis; the deal warrants its own dedicated analysis.

## Conclusion

Netflix is an excellent business, but excellence is already reflected in the price. With the base case and the comparable-company read both well below ~$110, and only the bull scenario clearing it, the risk-reward at today's level is unattractive. A disciplined investor would wait for a lower entry price or for clear evidence that results will exceed the base case.
